Artes Meae: Crafting My Path in Software Engineering and Academia

The goal of this essay is to encapsulate my professional philosophy across the interconnected realms of education, software development, and research. I approach these domains through two key metaphors: the magic of craftsmanship and the chaos of interdependent systems.

Software engineering, to me, is a form of magic—a craft where imagination is transformed into functional creations through code. As Frederick P. Brooks Jr. describes, programming is like "living magic," where creativity meets reality, and elegant solutions evoke a sense of beauty and wonder. This magic also extends to communication in education, where I am continually amazed by the process of transferring thoughts from one mind to another via language. It is a wonder how mere vibrations in the air, shaped by intent and communication, can convey knowledge so profoundly—something that fascinates me as a conlanger.

At the same time, the balance between education, academia, and industry resembles the three-body problem in physics—a system that is inherently unpredictable, lacking a simple solution. Much like the chaotic interplay described in Cixin Liu’s The Three-Body Problem, these three aspects exert influence on each other in complex ways, creating challenges that require adaptability and perseverance.

This essay will explore these themes, illustrating the magical aspects of craftsmanship and the chaotic dynamics of balancing different professional roles, while drawing on the concept of ergodic literature—a term derived from Greek meaning "work" (ergon) and "path" (hodos)—as a metaphor for the active and often non-linear journey of research and exploration.

Ars Paedagogiae: The Art of Education

Education forms the cornerstone of all professional pursuits—it is where the foundation for expertise is laid and where the seeds of curiosity are cultivated. However, true education is far more nuanced than the transfer of explicit knowledge from teacher to student. It is a process of discovery, guided by intuition, dialogue, and a commitment to growth.

The power of education lies in its ability to change perspectives, open doors, and unlock potentials that would otherwise remain dormant. But for it to truly have this transformative power, it must engage both the teacher and the student in a dynamic exchange, where each contributes to the other’s growth. This active exchange parallels the principles of ergodic literature, where active engagement with the text is necessary to create meaning. In the classroom, this interaction between teacher and student forms the basis of mutual growth and learning.

Ars Mechanica: The Craft of Software Engineering

Software engineering, much like education, requires a balance of craft and intuition. It involves finding elegant solutions to complex problems, but it also requires the patience to work through the less glamorous aspects of development—debugging, testing, and iteration. Like a craftsman perfecting a piece of art, the engineer must continuously hone their skills, paying attention to both the larger vision and the minute details.

The craftsmanship in software engineering lies in the ability to design systems that are not only functional but also maintainable, scalable, and user-friendly. Achieving this requires not just technical knowledge but also an understanding of the human element—how users will interact with the system and how it will evolve over time.

Moreover, software development is rarely a linear process. It often involves navigating through uncertainty, making trade-offs, and iterating on solutions until the final product emerges. This mirrors the chaotic, unpredictable nature of the three-body problem, where the interaction between different forces creates a system that is constantly in flux. Navigating this complexity requires both creativity and resilience.

Ars Investigationis: The Science of Research

Research is an integral part of both education and software engineering, providing the foundation for innovation and advancement. However, research, like the other aspects of my professional journey, is often unpredictable and chaotic. It involves exploring the unknown, asking questions that have no easy answers, and pushing the boundaries of what is possible.

Just as in the three-body problem, the forces at play in research are often difficult to predict. Success is not guaranteed, and progress is often slow. Yet, it is through this process of exploration and experimentation that new ideas are born and new knowledge is created.

This journey also parallels the concept of ergodic literature, where the reader must actively engage, explore, and make decisions that shape their experience. Similarly, my path as an educator, software developer, and researcher requires a continuous willingness to confront the chaos, make decisions despite the uncertainty, and adapt as the situation evolves.

Despite the frustration that often accompanies this unpredictability, the creative freedom to explore new ideas, challenge boundaries, and push beyond the known is what resonates with me most. The chaos is real, but so is the opportunity for exploration, which ultimately defines my professional journey.
